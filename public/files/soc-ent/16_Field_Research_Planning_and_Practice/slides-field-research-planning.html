Part 2

Building a Robust Research Protocol

Core Components of a Protocol

🎯 Learning Goals

What specific assumptions or questions are you trying to answer?

  • Tied to your Logic Model
  • Specific and testable

🔧 Method Choice

Which method best fits each learning goal?

  • Interviews, observation, surveys
  • Justify your selection

👥 Target Participants

Who will you engage with and how many?

  • Specific stakeholder groups
  • Sampling criteria

📝 Interview Guide / Checklist

Your prepared questions or observation framework

  • Open-ended questions
  • Probing follow-ups

📅 Logistics

Where, when, and how will you conduct the research?

  • Scheduling and location
  • Recording and note-taking plan

⚖ Ethical Considerations

How will you protect your participants?

  • Informed consent
  • Privacy and data handling

Recruitment & Logistics

Finding and engaging the right participants

🔍 Who to Engage

  • Direct beneficiaries of your venture
  • Community partners and gatekeepers
  • People who chose NOT to use similar services
  • Frontline workers in the space

📨 How to Find Them

  • Partner with community organizations
  • Ask for warm introductions
  • Visit places where your stakeholders gather
  • Leverage social media and local networks

💬 Before the Conversation

  • Explain your purpose clearly
  • Confirm time commitment (15-30 min)
  • Offer flexibility on location and format
  • Prepare your consent process

⚠ Common Pitfalls

  • Only talking to people who are easy to reach
  • Forgetting to include skeptics or non-users
  • Assuming one stakeholder speaks for all
  • Not planning for no-shows

🤖 AI as a Scenario Simulator

"Even the best-laid plans encounter obstacles in the field. We're going to use AI as a risk simulator to stress-test your protocols and make your research plans more resilient before you go out."

🤖 The Stress-Test Prompt

I am a student social entrepreneur preparing for a phase of primary field research. Here is my research protocol: Research Goal: [STATE YOUR SPECIFIC LEARNING GOAL] Method: [INTERVIEW / OBSERVATION / OTHER] Target Participants: [WHO AND HOW MANY] Recruitment Strategy: [HOW YOU'LL FIND THEM] Logistics: [WHERE, WHEN, HOW LONG] Act as a skeptical risk manager who has overseen dozens of failed field research projects. Your job is to help me prepare for what could go wrong. Please: 1. Brainstorm 3 plausible failure scenarios that could derail my data collection (e.g., participant no-shows, biased sample, logistical breakdowns). 2. For each scenario, suggest a specific contingency plan I can prepare in advance. 3. Identify one ethical risk I may not have considered and recommend how to address it.

💡 The Human Value-Add

AI generates scenarios. You decide what matters.

🧐 1. Evaluate

Review each AI-generated failure scenario critically.

  • Is this realistic for YOUR context?
  • Has the AI missed local knowledge?
  • What does the AI overestimate or underestimate?

🎯 2. Prioritize

Rank the scenarios by likelihood and severity.

  • Which 1-2 are most likely to happen?
  • Which would be most damaging?
  • What can you realistically prepare for?

🔧 3. Integrate

Build the best contingency plans into your protocol.

  • Add backup recruitment channels
  • Prepare alternative methods
  • Write a "Plan B" section

🎯 Research Planning Dilemmas

What would you do in these situations?

Scenario 1: The Cancelled Event

You planned to recruit participants at a community health fair next Saturday. Two days before, the organizer cancels the event due to weather. Your field research is due in two weeks. What do you do?

⏰ Ask your professor for an extension
🔄 Activate your backup recruitment plan
💻 Switch entirely to online surveys
😬 Wait and hope the event is rescheduled

✅ Best Choice: Activate your backup recruitment plan

This is exactly why your protocol includes contingency plans! Reach out to partner organizations, visit alternative community spaces, or leverage warm introductions from your network. A resilient protocol anticipates disruptions.

Scenario 2: The Biased Sample

After completing 4 of your 6 planned interviews, you realize all participants are college-educated professionals. Your social venture targets low-income families. Your data won't reflect your actual beneficiaries.

📝 Continue with current participants anyway
🔧 Revise your recruitment strategy for remaining interviews
🔄 Start over and discard all previous data
📊 Add a survey to compensate for the gap

✅ Best Choice: Revise your recruitment strategy

Don't discard useful data, but pivot your approach for the remaining interviews. Partner with community organizations that serve low-income families, visit locations where your target population gathers, and ask existing contacts for introductions. Acknowledge the sampling limitation in your analysis.
