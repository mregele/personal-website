Grounded Synthesis: Mindset over Method

We borrow the logic of Grounded Theory (Corbin & Strauss) — not the full academic apparatus.

Academic Rigor (Corbin & Strauss)

  • 📚 Months of iterative data collection
  • 📝 Formal open, axial & selective coding
  • 🔎 Theoretical sampling until saturation
  • 📋 Peer validation & member-checking

Built for academic dissertations and longitudinal research.

⚡ Entrepreneurial Speed (What We're Doing)

  • 🚀 Data-first: let what you heard lead
  • 🏷 Informal coding: name what you see, fast
  • 💡 Pattern-spotting across 5–10 conversations
  • 🤖 AI as synthesis partner, not as oracle

Built for ventures that need to pivot before next week.

The mindset is identical: you do not impose meaning on data — you let the pattern surface from what people actually said and did. The difference is speed, not integrity.

Part 1

From Notes to Patterns

The Synthesis Process

A four-stage path from noise to meaning

📄 Raw Notes

Everything you recorded — messy, unfiltered, verbatim

→

🏷 Coding

Assign short labels to each idea or observation (name what you see)

→

📚 Categorizing

Group similar codes; surface patterns and contradictions

→

💡 Insights

The "so what" — what does the pattern mean for your venture?

🔎 Look for

  • Repeated words or phrases
  • Moments of strong emotion
  • Things people avoided saying

⚠ Watch out for

  • Confirmation bias (only seeing what you expected)
  • Outliers you're tempted to dismiss

💬 Ask yourself

  • What surprised me most?
  • What made me uncomfortable?

Part 2

Stress-Testing the Assumptions

Confirming vs. Disconfirming Evidence

Honest researchers seek the data that threatens their model, not just the data that confirms it.

✅

Confirming Evidence

  • Data that aligns with your original assumptions
  • Observations that support your theory of change
  • User needs that match what you designed for
  • Behaviors you predicted actually occurring

Ask: Is this what I hoped to find, or what's actually there?

❌

Disconfirming Evidence

  • Data that contradicts a key assumption in your model
  • User behaviors that differ from what you expected
  • Barriers you hadn't accounted for
  • Needs that are different from the problem you're solving

Ask: What does this tell me that I didn't want to know?

📋 Activity: The Logic Model Audit

Pull up your Feb 24 Logic Model. Map your field evidence onto each box.

✅ Solid

Field evidence confirms this assumption. You have direct quotes or observations that support what you assumed.

⚠ Shaky

Mixed evidence — some confirms, some challenges. This assumption needs more investigation before you can trust it.

❌ Broken

Field evidence contradicts this assumption. This box may need to be rewritten or removed entirely.

💡 A Data Gap is an Insight

It tells you exactly where your venture’s biggest blindspots are. List these gaps as your top priorities for next week’s fieldwork. An honest gap is far more valuable than a weak assumption disguised as evidence.

🎯 Your Task

Go box by box (Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact). For each one, assign a status and write one piece of field evidence (a quote or observation) that justifies your rating. Which boxes are now "shaky"?

Part 3

Identifying the Catalyst

⚡ What is a Catalyst?

A catalyst is a powerful quote or vivid observation that forces a pivot — a single moment from your fieldwork so undeniable that it changes how you see the problem, the user, or your solution.

📹 What it looks like

  • A user said something that reframed everything
  • You observed a workaround that revealed a hidden need
  • A gap between what people said and what they did

🎯 What it does

  • Makes your assumptions feel naïve
  • Points toward a different target user
  • Reveals a better point of intervention

📋 Assignment 3 connection

  • Your catalyst becomes the opening of your Field Research Narrative
  • It justifies why your redesign is grounded in evidence

🔬 Diagnostic: Dealing with Thin Data

Being honest about what you don’t know is the key to a high grade on Assignment 3.

Data Problem What It Looks Like What to Do About It
🚫 Motivation Gap
Missing the ‘Why’ 		You know what people do but not why. Your notes describe behaviors without capturing the underlying needs, frustrations, or values driving them. Name it explicitly in your Narrative. Plan follow-up “Five Whys” interviews. Don’t fabricate motivation — a stated gap is honest research.
⚠ Critical Blindspots
Missing Logic Model Boxes 		Entire sections of your Logic Model have no evidence at all — you never accessed stakeholders who could speak to those links (e.g., never talked to end beneficiaries). Flag these as “Data Gap” in your Audit. List the specific stakeholders you still need to reach. This is not a failure — it is a roadmap.
🤖 Comfort Bias
Missing Disconfirming Evidence 		All your quotes confirm your assumptions. You spoke only to supportive stakeholders, or unconsciously recorded only what aligned with your original idea. Acknowledge the selection bias. Actively seek one skeptic or resistant user before submitting. Your Narrative should show you looked for challenge.

🏆 Grading note: A student who honestly identifies their data gaps and explains what they would do next scores higher than one who papers over thin evidence with confident-sounding claims.

🧠 Reflection: Your Role as Researcher

Take 5 minutes to write brief responses to these prompts in your notes.

Prompt 1 — Positionality

"How might your background, identity, or prior assumptions have shaped what you noticed in the field — and what you may have filtered out?"

Prompt 2 — Ambiguity

"What part of your data is still genuinely unclear? What are you tempted to resolve prematurely — and why is sitting with ambiguity actually the more honest choice?"

Prompt 3 — Ethical Responsibility

"You are making claims about people’s lives based on limited observation. What obligations do you have to represent your participants accurately and fairly?"

Prompt 4 — The Pivot

"If your fieldwork requires you to significantly revise your venture concept, how do you feel about that? What makes pivoting courageous rather than a failure?"

Assignment 3: Fieldwork Debrief & Reflection

  • Task 1 — Field Research Narrative — The messy story of your fieldwork journey: what you expected, what actually happened, and the moment that changed how you saw the problem.
  • Task 2 — Challenging Assumptions — Present disconfirming evidence directly. Which Logic Model boxes are “shaky” or “broken,” and what specific data demands that conclusion?
  • Task 3 — The Redesign — Identify your catalyst quote or observation, name the pivot it forces, and articulate your revised venture direction with evidence.
  • Task 4 — Researcher Reflection — Examine your biases, your positionality, and what this process revealed about you as a researcher navigating ambiguity.
