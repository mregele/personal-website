Escaping the Echo Chamber

What you already did

Fieldwork

  • Understanding the problem from the people who live it
  • Listening to users, clients, and community members
  • Grounding your venture in real human experience
  • Question: What is actually going on here?

What you are doing now

External Touchpoints

  • Validating your solution and business model with ecosystem experts
  • Pressure-testing assumptions with funders, domain specialists, and gatekeepers
  • Accessing knowledge, networks, and resources outside the classroom
  • Question: Will this actually work in the world?

Who Is the “Expert”?

🏭

Domain Experts

People with deep knowledge of the field, sector, or population your venture serves. They understand what works, what has been tried, and where the real constraints are.

e.g., non-profit directors, social workers, program managers, subject-matter faculty
💰

Resource Holders

People who control money, partnerships, or capacity that your venture needs to survive or scale. They evaluate viability and return — financial or social.

e.g., grant officers, foundation program staff, impact investors, corporate sponsors
🔑

Gatekeepers

People who control access to the communities, systems, or infrastructure your venture depends on. Without their buy-in, you simply cannot operate.

e.g., city officials, school principals, hospital administrators, community association leaders

The 1-Minute Intro & The Artifact

💬 Your 1–2 Sentence Introduction

You have roughly 60 seconds to establish who you are, what you are building, and why you are sitting across from this person. Experts are busy. If your intro is vague or wandering, the meeting is already lost.

Format: “We are building [X] to address [problem] for [population]. We are here to learn [specific question or concern].”

📄 The Lightweight Artifact

Bring something tangible to ground the conversation. A single page or one visual prevents the expert from having to imagine your venture — they can react to something concrete.

  • A 1-pager summarizing the problem and your model
  • A simplified logic model showing inputs, activities, and outcomes
  • A rough prototype, mockup, or pilot data summary

The artifact is not a sales pitch — it is a conversation starter. The goal is to make it easier for the expert to give you useful feedback, not to impress them.

Before Every Expert Meeting

Never take a meeting without a goal.

What 2–3 pieces of information could fundamentally change your venture — its model, its target population, its strategy, or whether to pursue it at all?

Write your learning goals before you write your questions. Questions without goals produce interesting conversations. Goals produce actionable insight.

What a Learning Goal Looks Like

Venture: After-school reading program

Goal: Determine the specific transportation barriers parents face at 4:00 PM.

Venture: Food waste logistics

Goal: Discover the exact financial threshold where grocery managers choose to donate rather than dump B-grade produce.

Notice the specificity: a good learning goal names a measurable condition, a specific actor, and a decision point — not just a topic area.

Crafting the Ask

❌ Questions to Avoid
“Would you fund an app that does X?”
“Do you think our idea is good?”
✔ Better Questions
“What is the biggest barrier you face when funding new tech?”
“Where do you see the biggest risk in a model like this?”

The rule: Bad questions ask the expert to validate you. Good questions ask the expert to share what they know. Put the expertise in the room to work.

Part 3

The Expert Simulation

Before you sit across from a real expert, sit across from an AI that plays one. Use it as a sparring partner to sharpen your pitch, stress-test your questions, and surface blind spots before they cost you.

🤖 The AI Role-Play Prompt

Copy this prompt into your AI tool. Fill in the bracketed fields before you begin.

Act as a [Insert Specific Expert Persona]. I am a social entrepreneur. I will give you a brief introduction to my venture and ask you a few questions. Please respond as the persona would, with realistic feedback, questions, and potential critiques. Do not break character.

Be specific with the persona

Instead of “a grant officer,” try: “a program officer at a Midwest community foundation focused on food security and workforce development.”

Deliver your real intro

Give the AI the same 1–2 sentence introduction you practiced. Then ask your three learning goal questions one at a time.

✍ Work Time

Run your AI expert simulation — deliver your intro, ask your questions, take notes on what surprises you.

30:00

While you work:

  • › Deliver your 1–2 sentence venture intro to the AI
  • › Ask your 2–3 learning goal questions one at a time
  • › Note critiques, surprises, and questions you had not anticipated

The Human Value-Add

Now analyze what just happened in your simulation.

  • Did the AI raise questions you had not anticipated? Which ones landed hardest?
  • Which of your questions fell flat — generated generic answers instead of specific insight?
  • Where did the AI's response reveal a gap in your venture logic that your team has been avoiding?
  • What would a real expert know that the AI clearly did not know?

The AI gives you a safe space to fail before it counts. Your job now is to use what you learned to make the real meeting sharper.
