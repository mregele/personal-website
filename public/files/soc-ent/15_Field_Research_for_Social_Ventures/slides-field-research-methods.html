🤖 AI Activity: Selecting Your Method

Use AI to Compare Research Methods

I am a social entrepreneur and student researcher. My primary learning goal for my next phase of field research is: [STATE YOUR SPECIFIC LEARNING GOAL HERE] Act as an experienced qualitative researcher and ethnographer. Please suggest three different field research methods I could use to achieve my learning goal. For each method, please: 1. Briefly describe how I would apply it in this context. 2. Analyze its primary 'pro' (what it's best at revealing). 3. Analyze its primary 'con' (its main limitation or what it might miss). Present this as a simple comparison table.

Remember: AI is a starting point. Always adapt questions to your specific context and validate with real conversations.

💡 Key Insight

"There is no single 'best' way to learn from the real world. The method you choose depends entirely on what you need to learn."

👁 Observation Methods

Fly-on-the-Wall

Watch without participating or interacting. You're invisible - just recording what happens naturally.

When to Use

  • You want to see uninfluenced behavior
  • Your presence might change how people act
  • You're observing public spaces or group dynamics
Example: Sitting in a community center lobby to see how people navigate services

Contextual Inquiry

Observe while asking questions in real-time. You're a curious apprentice learning from an expert (your user).

When to Use

  • You need to understand WHY they do things
  • The process is complex or has invisible steps
  • You want to catch workarounds and frustrations
Example: Watching a caseworker process applications while asking "What are you checking for here?"

Part 2

Mastering Interviews

Three Types of Interviews

Structured

Fixed questions in fixed order. Like a verbal survey.

  • Good for: Comparing responses
  • Bad for: Discovery
  • Flexibility: None
RECOMMENDED

Semi-Structured

Prepared topics with room to explore. Best of both worlds.

  • Good for: Customer discovery
  • Allows: Follow-up questions
  • Balance: Structure + flexibility

Unstructured

Free-flowing conversation with minimal guidance.

  • Good for: Early exploration
  • Risk: Going off-track
  • Requires: Expert interviewer

Anatomy of a Great Interview

1

Opening (2-3 min)

Build rapport and set expectations. Explain the purpose.

"Thanks for meeting with me. I'm researching how people manage their household finances. There are no right or wrong answers."
2

Context Questions (5-7 min)

Understand their situation before diving into problems.

"Walk me through a typical month of managing your finances..."
3

Core Exploration (15-20 min)

Dig into pain points, behaviors, and motivations.

"You mentioned that was frustrating. Can you tell me more about that?"
4

Closing (2-3 min)

Thank them and leave the door open for follow-up.

"Is there anything else you think would be helpful for me to know?"

Your Interview Guide

✘ Weak Guide

1. Do you like our product idea?
2. Would you pay $20/month?
3. How often would you use it?
4. What features do you want?

Leading questions that seek validation, not truth

✔ Strong Guide

1. Tell me about the last time you faced [problem]...
2. How do you currently handle that?
3. What's most frustrating about it?
4. What have you tried? What happened?

Open questions that uncover real behavior and pain

⚠ Common Interview Pitfalls

💬 Leading Questions

"Don't you think this would be helpful?"

Fix: "How do you currently handle this?"

📢 Pitching Solutions

Explaining your idea before listening

Fix: Focus on their problem first, save your solution for later

🔮 Hypothetical Questions

"Would you use an app that..."

Fix: "Tell me about the last time you..."

🎯 Powerful Probing Questions

Clarification
  • "What do you mean by that?"
  • "Can you give me an example?"
  • "Help me understand..."
Depth
  • "Why was that important to you?"
  • "How did that make you feel?"
  • "What happened next?"
Contrast
  • "How is this different from...?"
  • "What would the ideal look like?"
  • "Compare this to your old approach"

👂 Active Listening Tips

  • Embrace silence - Pause after they finish. They often add more.
  • Mirror their words - "So what I'm hearing is..."
  • Note emotions - "You seem frustrated by that."
  • Avoid judgment - Don't react positively or negatively
  • Stay curious - Follow unexpected threads that emerge

🎯 Scenario Showdown

Which method would YOU choose?

Scenario 1

You're developing a meal delivery service for elderly residents. You want to understand the daily challenges they face with current food options. You haven't talked to any potential users yet.

🎤 Interviews
📊 Surveys
👁 Observation
🧪 Prototype Test

✅ Best Choice: Interviews

At this early stage, you need to understand the "why" behind their challenges. Interviews let you explore their stories, motivations, and pain points in depth. Surveys would be premature since you don't yet know what questions to ask.

🎯 Scenario Showdown

Scenario 2

Your nonprofit runs tutoring sessions for high schoolers. Attendance has dropped, but when you ask students why, they say "everything's fine." You suspect something else is happening.

🎤 Interviews
📊 Surveys
👁 Observation
🧪 Prototype Test

✅ Best Choice: Observation

When what people say doesn't match reality, you need to see what's actually happening. Observing sessions might reveal issues students aren't aware of or won't admit - uncomfortable seating, awkward group dynamics, or transportation issues.

Scenario 3

Based on 15 interviews, you believe parents would pay $50/month for your after-school program. Your assumption is clear, and now you need data from 100+ families.

🎤 Interviews
📊 Surveys
👁 Observation
🧪 Prototype Test

✅ Best Choice: Surveys

You've done the exploratory work and have a specific hypothesis. Now a survey can efficiently validate whether this price point works across a larger sample. The key: you earned the right to survey by doing interviews first!

📝 Key Takeaways

  • Start with interviews - They're your most powerful discovery tool
  • Ask about the past - "Tell me about the last time..." not "Would you..."
  • Listen more, talk less - Your job is to learn, not convince
  • Follow the unexpected - The best insights come from surprise
  • Combine methods - Observe, interview, then validate with data
